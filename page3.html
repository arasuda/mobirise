Why It Is Important to Me

A descritipon of why i think blood donationis important and the goals of this project

Abstract 

Blood donation is a global problem, particularly in the UK. The fact that only 4% of the UK population are registered to donate blood, and even less than that donate blood. This is in stark contrast to the fact that more than 40% of the population gives blood. According to the NHS, 135,000 donors are needed every year; this goal has been reached in the past few years. However, even although the target number of donors is reached every year, the number of people who donate is significantly smaller. Only recently, at the start of this project, there has been an outbreak of the coronavirus (COVID-19). This, in turn, has led to a decree in donations and donors because of COVID-19. This is a current problem as the amount of blood that the NHS requires every year has not decreased. Most noticeably white blood cell to help people that have naturally weak immune systems to fight the virus. Because of the outbreak, not only as an epidemic but in this case a pandemic, the NHS is in constant need of people to donate blood, not only to test patients' blood to see how it may respond to the virus.


Nevertheless, to help treat people that may have the virus. Even if there is a current pandemic affecting the world, the NHS still requires blood regularly; because if someone is in a severe car accident, they may require up to 100 pints of blood and the average person can only donate up to 1 pint every for months. As a result, this means that there could be shortages of blood in the future if people give regularly. This, in turn, would lead to people who have a disease like leukaemia loving a much shorter life or having a reduced quality of life.


Wider context

Across the Uk, there has been a decrease in the number of people wanting to donate blood. Of 2,126 people surveyed for why they did not donate blood (2016 NHS survey): 20% believed that they could not donate because they have tattoos, 16% because of piercings, 15% because of smoking and 3 % because of their diet.


Nationally more and more misconceptions are being spread about blood donation, which has led to less and fewer people either requesting or donating bloodâ€”or reducing the number of times that they donate. This harms the number of people that require blood transfusions. It is a common misconception that people receive blood transfusions because they have had a severe injury; however, people require them for many different reasons such as cancer patients, people who suffer from sickle cell syndrome and women in labour. In most cases of blood donation, it is not to save a person's life, but it is to improve the overall quality of that person's life. 


According to the NHS Blood and transplant, it is estimated only 5% of the UK's black, Asien and minority ethnics population donate blood; this is only slightly higher than the national average while the BAME population only consists of less than 20%. Is an issue because there are many different blood-related illnesses such as sickle cell syndrome Littlemore prominent in Minority communities. Especially in black communities. People in the BAME population should donate blood because certain illnesses are more treatable with the blood of someone from a similar demographic. This is because some illnesses are only present in specific communities, or there is a significantly lower chance of catching this illness if a person is from a different heritage to white British people. 


Specific context 

The objective of these assignments is just knowledge to the public about misconceptions to do with blood donation as well as the benefits of doing so. not just for the person receiving the blood for the person donating it as well. As of 2019, The number of male donors has decreased by almost 25% due to multiple factors, two of which being men are more likely to be afraid of needles (20%) and giving blood (24%) in comparison to women (Hannah Parrots July 11 2019). A fear of needles is prevalent amongst young people growing up that affects many children as well as young adults. However, it is not the needle going into their arm that scares them; it is the thought of how much it hurts. When in reality, most time and needle is involved, the pain is very minute and only lasts a small period. However, this is a strange Factor because men are afraid I am giving blood even though statistically, men are less afraid of blood than women. This is because men are more prone to injury at work, whether a person in sport or day to day life. 


When it comes to the donation aspect, more and more doctors are using online social media to explain common misconceptions with blood donation as well as showing people how simple it is for them to register. There are "only four steps, registration, health check, donating of the actual blood and snacks" (doctor mike). Subtracting the registration process that can no longer be filled out in the blood donation centre itself, complete online at home, all other processes can be completed on the day at the blood donation centre. It is usually in the course of about 10 - 20 minutes. This is in stark contrast to the fallacy that blood donation takes a considerable period to register or to donate blood. 


In 2018, there was a decrease in the amount of blood that the NHS had. This shortage happened in only two days due to the lack of donors leading up to that bank holiday. This put a massive strain on the NHS as they had to use their supplies of O - blood because they did not have enough Ro blood to reach the demand that was needed. Even though in July 7,000 people volunteered in last year's blood drive, to establish the amount of blood available to the country. It still was not enough as the NHS needed 40,000 black donors alone.  


The work 

Because NHS blood and transplant Is a government-run charity organisation, this means that any blood donations would be a voluntary act from the general public. Not showing a constant advertisement to the public could lead to a decrease in the number of people that donate every year. As a result, because awareness is not being spread at schools and universities, this has led to the NHS having to use ways to persuade people to donate blood. Most of these methods involve forms of advertisement such as new forms of advertisement that have been used on Facebook, YouTube and Twitter. It was reported in June of 2019 there was a reduction in males donors, up to 25% in over five years. This has been a significant problem as men can donate every 12 weeks, in comparison to women then can donate every 16 weeks. As a result, men can donate 4 pints of blood a year, while women can only donate 3. Not only can men donate more frequently, but men's blood is less likely to cause reactions in patients, it is more stable for platelet and plasma transfusions.


Because of the decline in the number of men that are donating each year, the NHS blood has focused most of its blood donation advertisements into advertisements that incorporate men/masculinity.
